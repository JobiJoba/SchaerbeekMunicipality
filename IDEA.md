This is the answer:

Below is how I would explain the process to a new manager joining the Population Department of a Belgian municipality. The exact implementation varies slightly between municipalities and depending on nationality (EU, non-EU, refugee, diplomat, etc.), but this reflects the general administrative workflow used in Belgium.

---

# High-Level Goal

The purpose of a first registration is **not simply to create a record**.

The municipality must determine:

1. Who is this person?
2. Are they legally allowed to reside in Belgium?
3. Do they actually live at the declared address?
4. Under which legal status must they be registered?
5. Which other administrations must be informed?

Only after those questions are answered does the person officially become part of the Belgian population registers.

# Major Exceptions

## Missing Birth Certificate

Registration may be delayed until:

Translation

Legalisation

Replacement documents

Court decision

---

## Marriage Not Recognised

Person may temporarily remain registered as single.

Recognition follows another procedure.

---

## Police Cannot Confirm Residence

Registration may be refused.

Further investigation begins.

---

## Duplicate Identity

The municipality cannot simply create another person.

Investigation is opened.

---

## Illegal Stay

Population department cannot regularise immigration status.

Case is handled under immigration law.

---

## Refugees

Additional decisions from federal authorities determine the applicable registration process.

---

## EU Citizens

Different documentation requirements apply than for non-EU nationals.

---

## Diplomats

Registration follows separate rules.

---

## Homeless Persons

Specific procedures exist, including reference addresses or registration through designated mechanisms, depending on the circumstances.

# The Core Decision Process

From an administrative perspective, nearly every first registration comes down to four sequential questions:

1. **Can we establish the person's identity with sufficient certainty?**
2. **Do they have a legal basis to reside in Belgium under the applicable immigration rules?**
3. **Do they genuinely reside at the declared address within the municipality?**
4. **Based on those facts, in which register should they be entered, and what legal status should be recorded?**

Everything else—document collection, police verification, communication with other authorities, issuance of certificates, and residence card production—supports those four determinations. Once they are all resolved positively, the municipality can complete the registration and the person becomes officially registered in Belgium.
